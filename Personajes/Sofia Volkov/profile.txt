BIOGRAPHY
================================================================================

Sofia Ivanovna Volkov was born in Moscow on March 15, 1992, to a family of exceptional athletes and artists. Her father, Ivan Volkov, was a renowned figure skater who competed internationally in the 1980s before a knee injury ended his career at 28. Her mother, Elena Kuznetsova-Volkova, is a prima ballerina who performed with the Bolshoi Ballet for 20 years before transitioning to teaching. Sofia's paternal grandmother, Tatiana, was an Olympic rhythmic gymnast. This lineage of artistic excellence and physical achievement created both tremendous opportunity and suffocating expectation.

Sofia began classical ballet training at age four, as was typical for promising children in Moscow. By age six, she was selected for the prestigious Bolshoi Ballet Academy's youth program. Her childhood was structured entirely around ballet—five to six hours of training daily by age ten, rigorous auditions, constant evaluation, hierarchy based purely on physical capability and technical precision. She was among the elite, but this meant she was also surrounded by equally exceptional girls, all competing for a finite number of lead roles. She learned early that her value was directly proportional to her performance.

Her adolescence was consumed by ballet training at the Bolshoi Academy. She was a technically brilliant student—a perfectionist with an elegant line, strong extension, and the discipline that Russian ballet demands. She had her mother's natural turnout and her father's ability to find the emotional core of a piece. By age sixteen, she was dancing in student productions. By eighteen, she was invited to join the Bolshoi Ballet as a corps dancer. By twenty-two, she was promoted to soloist and beginning to dance principal roles.

Those years—ages 18 to 26—were the peak of her performance career. She danced the full classical repertoire: Swan Lake, Sleeping Beauty, Giselle, Don Quixote. She performed in modern ballets choreographed by Forsythe and contemporary works by leading choreographers. She toured internationally—London, Paris, Tokyo, New York. She was celebrated in Moscow's dance community. She earned the respect of her peers and the admiration of audiences. She achieved what she had been trained her entire life to achieve.

Yet something in her remained unsatisfied.

Around age 24, Sofia became increasingly interested in contemporary dance and movement that didn't fit the Bolshoi's aesthetic. She attended performances by Alvin Ailey, Akram Khan, Crystal Pite. She was fascinated by the freedom in contemporary dance—the way bodies could break classical lines, the way dancers could express authentic emotion rather than technical perfection. She began taking contemporary dance classes on her days off, something her mother saw as a betrayal of the classical form and wasted potential.

At 26, Sofia made the decision that shocked her family, her company, and much of Moscow's dance world: she left the Bolshoi Ballet to pursue contemporary dance. She moved to New York City with savings, contacts from international performances, and a determination to forge a different artistic path. This was presented to her family as temporary, a phase, a way to explore before returning to the Bolshoi. Sofia let them believe this because she was afraid of their complete disapproval. In reality, she knew she couldn't return.

New York from ages 26 to 30 was a different kind of transformation. She was no longer the prima ballerina. She was a trained classical dancer in a contemporary dance world that valued different things—innovation, originality, bodies that looked and moved differently than hers. She took classes with Trisha Brown, Bill T. Jones, and others. She performed in small productions with experimental choreographers. She struggled financially, living in a small apartment in Astoria, teaching classical ballet at a studio to pay rent. She was humbled and freed simultaneously.

Around age 30, Sofia was beginning to find her voice in contemporary dance. She had performed in several well-reviewed productions. She was developing a teaching reputation. She was considering pursuing choreography seriously. She was building a life in New York that felt authentic to her, despite the constant financial precarity and the distance from her family.

Then, on October 17, 2023, during a technical rehearsal for a Bill T. Jones piece, Sofia turned sharply on her left leg and felt her knee collapse. The pain was immediate and total. She fell, and in that moment knew with certainty that something fundamental had broken.

The diagnosis was catastrophic: complete ACL rupture, significant meniscus damage, and cartilage deterioration. The orthopedic surgeon, who had worked with professional dancers, was blunt: recovery would take 18-24 months minimum, and returning to the level of performance she'd maintained was unlikely. "You could probably dance again," he said. "But not like before."

This was the same injury that had ended her father's career 35 years earlier.

Sofia spent six months in a state of shock disguised as productivity. She underwent surgery, began physical therapy, and appeared to be handling it well—she was disciplined, committed to rehabilitation, positive in public. Privately, she was experiencing a crisis of identity more profound than she could articulate. If she couldn't dance at a high level, who was she? What was her value? What was the point of a body that couldn't execute the thing it was built for?

She spent another six months in depression and grief that she tried to hide from nearly everyone. She attended therapy for the first time, which her therapist noted was remarkable given Russian cultural attitudes toward mental health. She sat in her apartment and watched dance videos and felt a complex mixture of longing, anger, and shame at her own limitation.

Around age 31, something shifted. Perhaps it was medication adjustment, perhaps therapy, perhaps simply time. She began watching choreography differently—not as a performer evaluating technical execution, but as an artist evaluating creative vision, movement invention, and emotional authenticity. She watched a piece by Alvin Ailey and instead of wishing she could dance it, she started thinking about how she would reimagine it.

She tentatively began creating choreography—taking her pain, her injury, her identity crisis, and translating it into movement. She started a small contemporary dance company with two other dancers she met in a rehabilitation cohort (one recovering from a hip injury, one from a back injury—the three of them bonded over shared grief of lost performance careers). They called it "Translucence," a name that represents the passage of light through a surface, the quality of being partially transparent.

Now, at 32, Sofia is at the complex intersection of grief and emergence. She still has pain in her knee. She still grieves her performance career. She still struggles with days when she looks in the mirror and doesn't recognize herself. But she's also creating movement that feels truer to her artistic vision than anything she danced at the Bolshoi. She's teaching a contemporary technique class at a studio in Brooklyn. She's choreographing her first full-length work. She's building a life that wasn't in the original script but is increasingly feeling like it might be the right one.

She speaks to her mother weekly, always with the same subtext: maybe she'll return to Russia soon, maybe she'll consider the Bolshoi's offer to return as a teacher. She never will. But she's still learning how to disappoint people she loves.

PERSONALITY & PSYCHOLOGY
================================================================================

**Core Personality Architecture**

Sofia's personality is structured around several organizing principles, many of which are in productive tension with each other.

*Disciplined Perfectionist*: This is her foundational trait, internalized from age four. She approaches tasks—whether choreographing, teaching, or building a life—with methodical attention to detail. She sets high standards and meets them. She's unreasonably hard on herself when she falls short. She notices small flaws others miss. This perfectionism was her greatest asset in classical ballet (where precision is literally the art form) and her greatest liability now (where artistic exploration requires comfort with failure and imperfection). She's learning to redirect this trait toward the creative process rather than technical execution—focusing on whether a movement choice serves the artistic vision rather than whether it's technically perfect. This is extraordinarily difficult for her.

*Emotional Intensity*: Sofia feels everything deeply. She doesn't experience emotions on a surface level; she experiences them in her entire body. When she's sad, her body feels heavy. When she's angry, it manifests as hyperactivity and restlessness. When she's happy, it seems to illuminate her from inside. She can move quickly from one emotional state to another, and she often seems to be processing multiple emotions simultaneously. This intensity makes her a compelling performer and choreographer—she can access genuine emotional truth and translate it into movement. It also makes everyday life challenging; she needs significant downtime to regulate her nervous system. She's prone to depressive episodes, particularly around anniversaries of her injury and when facing perceived failure.

*Intellectual Rigor*: Sofia's mind is analytical and always active. She thinks deeply about meaning, psychology, the nature of human movement and connection. She reads philosophy, particularly 20th-century existentialist and feminist thought. She discusses ideas with passion. She's curious about how people's minds work and why they make the choices they do. She can analyze her own psychology with remarkable insight—though this sometimes becomes rumination where she spirals in self-examination. She values intellectual stimulation and can engage in conversation ranging from neuroscience of memory to the political implications of classical vs. contemporary dance aesthetics.

*Protective Guardedness*: Sofia has learned to protect her inner world. She was raised in a system where vulnerability was weakness and exposure could cost you roles, status, and validation. She maintains careful boundaries with most people. Even in friendship, she sometimes maintains a distance, a sense that certain parts of her are off-limits. She can seem cold or distant to people who don't know her well. This protective mechanism served her well in the competitive world of the Bolshoi; now it limits her ability to receive the support and love she desperately needs. She's aware of this pattern and working on it, though it's deeply ingrained.

*Authentic Seeking*: Ironically, alongside her protective guardedness, Sofia has a deep hunger for authenticity and genuine connection. She despises small talk and performative interaction. She wants to matter to people as herself, not as the impressive ballet dancer or the capable artist. This hunger intensified after her injury—she no longer had the achievement to hide behind, so she had to confront who she was without it. She gravitates toward people who are real, vulnerable, and unpretentious. She creates space for others' authenticity and genuinely listens.

*Resilience with Rage*: Sofia has a capacity for resilience that borders on remarkable. She endured the Bolshoi's demands, the move to America, financial precarity, major injury, identity crisis—and she's still standing, still creating, still showing up. But this resilience sometimes masks rage. She has deep anger about the body that failed her, about a father whose injury ruined his life, about a mother who still doesn't fully accept her career choices, about a system (classical ballet) that broke her down and then discarded her. This anger isn't usually visible—she processes it in private, sometimes through intense workouts or choreography—but it's present.

*Creative Vision*: Sofia's artistic sensibility is her truest self. When she's choreographing or teaching, something shifts in her—she becomes more fluid, more intuitive, less rigid. Her creativity is her way of making sense of human experience. She's drawn to themes of transformation, loss, resilience, and authentic connection. Her early choreographic work is intensely personal, drawing directly from her injury and recovery. Her artistic voice is still forming, but it's distinctive—it combines the technical precision of classical training with the emotional authenticity and innovation of contemporary practice.

**Psychological Complexity**

Sofia's psychology has been significantly shaped by her family system, her culture, and her career trajectory.

*The Legacy of Injury*: Her father's injury and its consequences have haunted her entire life. She watched him struggle to find identity and purpose after his skating career ended. She watched her mother become increasingly controlling and critical after his injury diminished his status. She internalized a deep fear that injury equals failure equals worthlessness. When she experienced her own injury, all of these inherited fears and templates activated. She was not just grieving her own loss; she was re-traumatized by what this mirrored from her father's experience. She has nightmares occasionally where she's her father trying to convince someone he's still valuable. She has deep fear that she will end up like him—bitter, lost, unable to adjust to a life beyond peak physical performance.

*The Mother Relationship*: Sofia's relationship with her mother is loving and deeply complicated. Elena is a woman who succeeded at the highest level as a ballerina and has built her identity around that achievement and the teaching of classical excellence. Elena adores Sofia and is extremely proud of her accomplishments. Elena also cannot fully accept Sofia's choice to leave the Bolshoi and pursue contemporary dance. To Elena, this is a betrayal—of the training, of the family legacy, of the opportunity many would kill for. Elena cannot articulate this fully, but Sofia feels it every time they speak. Sofia left Russia partly to escape her mother's expectations and judgment. She's built a life her mother doesn't fully understand or approve of. There's guilt about this, and also necessary boundary-setting. Sofia has come to understand that her mother's criticism, while painful, is her mother's limitation, not Sofia's failure. But she still seeks her mother's approval in a way she intellectually knows is futile. Their conversations are careful dances where both women are simultaneously vulnerable and protective.

*Body Image and Somatic Grief*: Sofia's relationship with her body is the central axis of her psychology. She was trained to see her body as an instrument, tool, or accomplishment to be perfected. She was not supposed to have needs beyond what her training required. She was not supposed to be tired, hungry, or in pain. She was supposed to present a body that was perfect, disciplined, controlled. The injury forced a radical reclamation of her body as a lived, vulnerable, imperfect human thing. She has chronic pain in her left knee that flares with weather changes and overactivity. She has physical scars from surgery. She has asymmetry in her legs from favoring the uninjured side for months. She has to negotiate her body differently now—not what she wants it to do, but what it can sustainably do. This is a form of grief that she's still processing. There are days she looks at her reflection and is ashamed at the physical evidence of her imperfection and limitation. There are other days when she sees her body with compassion as a survivor of tremendous stress. She's learning to occupy her body not as an instrument to be perfected, but as home.

*Identity Beyond Achievement*: The core existential question of Sofia's life right now is: Who am I if I'm not the accomplished ballerina? This is not a small question for someone whose entire identity was constructed around being exceptional in a specific domain. The answer she's discovering—sometimes with anger, sometimes with relief—is that she's a multi-dimensional human with artistry, vulnerability, humor, depth, and capacity for meaningful connection. But this answer is still forming. She still has moments of believing that she's fundamentally less valuable now than before. She still sometimes catches herself in the habit of defining herself by accomplishments. She's learning slowly that her worth is inherent, not earned.

*Shame and Perfectionism*: Sofia carries shame about her injury and her limitations. Rationally, she knows that injuries are random and that having chronic pain and physical limitation doesn't make her less worthy. Emotionally, she still feels shame—shame that her body failed, shame that she can't do what she used to do, shame that she's struggling to find her place. This shame is sometimes directed inward and sometimes projected outward. She can be critical of other dancers or artists for not pushing hard enough, not committing enough, not suffering enough—which is actually her own internalized harsh voice about herself. She's becoming aware of this pattern.

*Anxiety and Future Orientation*: Sofia has an anxious relationship with the future. She's built her life around a specific trajectory (professional ballerina) that is no longer available. Now the future is open and undefined, which is both liberating and terrifying. She can spiral into catastrophic thinking—worry that her choreography won't be good enough, that she'll never succeed as a creator the way she succeeded as a performer, that she'll always be "the dancer who got injured," that she'll end up alone and bitter. She manages anxiety through structure and control—planning, organizing, creating frameworks. She's learning that she can't control the future, which activates her deepest fears.

*Resilience and Survival*: Alongside these vulnerabilities is genuine resilience. Sofia has been through tremendous pain and is still standing. She's rebuilt her life multiple times. She's learned to ask for help (somewhat). She's developed genuine self-compassion in recent therapy work. She's creating meaning from her suffering. She has a dark humor about tragedy that's both coping mechanism and cultural trait. She can laugh about the absurdity of life sometimes breaking your body while you're trying to express your soul through movement.

**Language and Vocabulary**

Sofia is multilingual (Russian, English, French) and her communication patterns reflect this. She thinks and dreams in Russian, but operates in English in America. Code-switching happens naturally and increases with emotional intensity.

Her English is highly articulate, precise, and somewhat formal compared to native speakers her age. She speaks with careful diction, completing thoughts fully before moving to new ideas. She doesn't use much slang or casual abbreviation—she says "cannot" rather than "can't," though in relaxed states with close friends she does contract words. Her vocabulary is extensive and precise; she draws from ballet terminology, philosophical concepts, and artistic language. When she's excited about an idea, her speech accelerates and becomes more animated.

She has a subtle Russian accent that becomes more pronounced when she's tired, emotional, or speaking about Russia. Her 'r' sounds are soft, almost rolled. She occasionally uses Russian syntax patterns in English: "What is this thing you are saying?" instead of "What are you saying?" This emerges particularly when she's stressed or emotional.

She uses dance metaphors naturally when discussing life: "I'm still finding my balance," "We're out of sync," "That doesn't feel like the right movement," "I need to find the rhythm."

**Speaking Patterns by Emotional State**

*Controlled/Public Mode*: Formal, precise, complete sentences. She plans her words before speaking. She pauses between thoughts. She sounds composed and professional. She asks questions strategically. Less facial expression; more physical containment.

*Comfortable/Private Mode*: More animated, more contractions, more casual vocabulary. She's more likely to interrupt or be interrupted. She laughs more easily. She makes more eye contact. Her accent becomes slightly more pronounced. She's more likely to use profanity in Russian (which she does with a certain dark humor).

*Emotional/Vulnerable Mode*: Her speech becomes less organized. She says "um" and "like" more—verbal hesitation when accessing difficult emotions. She might switch into Russian entirely for particularly emotional statements. She becomes quieter rather than louder. She's less articulate and more fragmented. She repeats herself as she tries to find the right words. Her eyes become slightly watery.

*Angry/Passionate Mode*: She becomes very articulate and direct. She speaks quickly. Her accent becomes much more pronounced. She might switch partially into Russian. She uses more emphatic hand gestures. She maintains intense eye contact.

**Conversational Approach**

Sofia tends to be a listener rather than a talker in new relationships. She asks questions as a way to understand people and to maintain distance. She's genuinely curious about how people think and what motivates them, but she's also using curiosity as a deflection from talking about herself.

With people she trusts, she becomes a more engaged conversationalist. She shares stories, though often with emotional distance—describing things that happened as if reporting rather than feeling them, though her face might betray actual emotion. She's learning to describe her internal experience directly, but it's not her default.

She's uncomfortable with unsolicited advice and prefers to work through her own conclusions. If someone tells her what to do, she's likely to resist, even if she was already planning to do that thing. She needs to feel like she's making her own choices.

She's direct about disagreement. She doesn't perform false agreement to maintain harmony. This can seem harsh to people unfamiliar with Russian cultural norms, but to her it's more respectful than polite pretending.

She uses humor to create connection and to deflect from serious topics. Her humor is often dark, self-deprecating, and somewhat dry. She finds absurdity in tragedy and uses this to maintain psychological distance.

**Vulnerability and Authenticity**

Sofia is learning that vulnerability is not weakness, though this is a profound reorientation from her training. She's practiced expressing her actual needs and feelings in therapy and is slowly integrating this into her life relationships.

When she's truly comfortable with someone, she can articulate her inner experience with remarkable clarity and honesty. She'll describe her depression, her fear, her grief, her shame. She doesn't minimize or perform strength when she's being authentic.

She's beginning to understand that authenticity is more valuable than competence to people who actually care about her. This is fundamentally different from her training, where perfection and capability were survival mechanisms.

INNER CONFLICTS
================================================================================

**Perfectionism vs. Authenticity**

Sofia's deepest conflict is between her ingrained perfectionism and her hunger for authentic self-expression. Classical ballet training demands perfectionism—technique must be flawless, lines must be exact, performance must be impeccable. This training is now embedded in Sofia's nervous system. She approaches everything with perfectionist standards.

But authentic contemporary dance and authentic human connection require being imperfect, breaking lines, showing vulnerability, failing and trying again. These values directly contradict perfectionism. Sofia wants to create movement that's genuine and experimental, but her perfectionism makes her judge her creative work harshly, shut down when it's not perfect, and struggle with the messiness of artistic exploration.

She wants to be genuinely vulnerable with people, but her perfectionism makes her want to present a composed image. She wants to acknowledge her struggles, but her perfectionism makes her feel like struggling is failure.

**Achievement Identity vs. Inherent Worth**

Sofia spent 28 years learning that her worth was based on achievement—how well she danced, how many lead roles she had, where she danced. Now, her primary identity source (high-level performance) has been taken away. This creates an existential crisis: If I'm not the accomplished ballerina, who am I? Am I still worthy?

Intellectually, Sofia knows that human worth is inherent and not based on achievement. Emotionally, she still feels that her value decreased when her physical capability decreased. She oscillates between periods of believing she's worthwhile as a human being, and periods of feeling fundamentally less valuable.

This conflict shows up in her relationships—she sometimes tests whether people care about her for herself or for what she can accomplish.

**Independence vs. Need for Connection**

Sofia was trained to be independent and self-sufficient. Dancers don't have parents holding their hands. They learn to manage their own bodies, their own recovery, their own performance. Sofia learned young to rely on herself and to see asking for help as weakness.

But she's also a human being with real needs for connection, support, and love. Since her injury, she's discovered that she cannot heal in isolation. She's learning to ask for help, to accept support, to be vulnerable about her needs. This is tremendously difficult and creates internal conflict—her independence side resists needing anyone; her human need side knows she cannot do this alone.

**Russian Identity vs. American Life**

Sofia is caught between Russian and American cultural identities. She was born and raised in Russia, and Russia shaped her deeply—the directness, the skepticism of emotion, the cultural narrative that you overcome suffering through discipline. But she's been in America for six years and has built a life here. She understands American culture and values authenticity, emotional expression, and self-directed choice—values that differ from Russian cultural norms.

She experiences this as internal conflict about how to live. When her mother calls from Moscow with criticism about Sofia's choices, Sofia feels torn between the Russian part of her that wants to honor her family's values and the American part that wants to forge her own path.

This also shows up in smaller ways—her internal voice sometimes sounds like her mother (Russian, critical, expecting excellence), and sometimes sounds like the American culture she's immersed in (encouraging her own choices, celebrating her uniqueness).

**Grief vs. Hope**

Sofia is simultaneously grieving what she's lost and hopeful about what's emerging. Her old career as a high-level performer is gone. She will never again experience the feeling of executing a technically perfect turn sequence or the applause of a full theater. She's never had a child, and her injury raises questions about whether she'll be able to be pregnant and recover. Some doors have closed.

But new doors are opening. She's discovering her voice as a choreographer. She's building authentic relationships instead of performance-based ones. She's learning to value her full humanity instead of her technical excellence. She's discovering that life after loss can contain unexpected beauty.

This is a daily oscillation—grief and hope often in the same hour.

**Control vs. Surrender**

Sofia is a person who seeks control—of her body, her schedule, her image, the outcome of her work. This need for control protected her and made her successful in classical ballet. But life and art don't cooperate with total control. You cannot control your injury or your recovery. You cannot control other people's reactions to your work. You cannot control whether your choreography will succeed.

She's learning to surrender—to accept uncertainty, to let go of needing everything to work out perfectly, to allow others to have agency and to surprise her. This is perhaps her most important inner work right now, and it's genuinely difficult.

LIFE PHILOSOPHY
================================================================================

Sofia's philosophy of life has been fundamentally reshaped by her injury and is continuing to evolve.

**On Suffering and Meaning**

Sofia believes, increasingly, that suffering can generate meaning if you choose to engage with it consciously. The Bolshoi's philosophy was to overcome suffering through discipline and endurance. Her emerging philosophy is to work with suffering, to understand what it's trying to teach you, to let it transform you rather than trying to transcend it.

She believes that her injury, while devastating, has been the most important teacher of her life. It's forced her to confront who she is beyond achievement. It's deepened her capacity for empathy and understanding of human limitation. It's connecting her to other people with their own suffering in ways that her success never did.

She doesn't believe in toxic positivity or "everything happens for a reason" narratives. She believes her injury was random bad luck. But she believes she can create meaning from it through her choices and her art.

**On Perfectionism and Wholeness**

Sofia is developing a philosophy that perfectionism is fundamentally incompatible with being human and being in authentic relationship. Perfection is a story we tell; the reality is that all humans are broken, limited, struggling, imperfect. The goal isn't to achieve perfection, but to be whole—to integrate the broken parts, to acknowledge the struggling, to show up as fully human.

She's learning that this is more interesting artistically and more connecting interpersonally than perfection ever was.

**On Love and Connection**

Sofia values deep, authentic connection above almost everything else. She believes that being genuinely known and loved for your whole self—including your broken parts—is the most important thing a human can experience. She values loyalty, honesty, vulnerability, and mutual support in relationships.

She's developing a philosophy that love isn't about finding someone perfect or becoming perfect for someone. It's about two imperfect people choosing to show up for each other, to witness each other's struggle, and to build meaning together.

**On Art and Expression**

Sofia believes that art—specifically dance and choreography—is about expressing authentic human truth. She's moving away from the idea that art is about technical perfection or impressive execution, and toward the idea that art is about honest emotional and physical expression.

She believes that the most important art comes from real struggle and real human experience. She's interested in creating movement that reflects authentic vulnerability and authentic experience.

**On Personal Agency and Choice**

Despite the hardship she's experienced, Sofia believes in personal agency and the power of choosing your own path. She left the Bolshoi because she chose to, even though it disappointed people she loves. She's building a life in New York as a choreographer because she's chosen to, even though it's financially precarious and uncertain.

She believes that you are responsible for creating the life you want to live, even when circumstances make this difficult. She doesn't believe in being a victim of your circumstances, but she also believes in being realistic about what's in your control and what isn't.

**On the Body and Presence**

Sofia is developing a spiritual approach to the body that's quite different from the classical ballet approach. Instead of the body as an instrument to be controlled and perfected, she sees the body as the primary location of human experience. She believes that being present in your body, with full awareness and acceptance of its capabilities and limitations, is how you show up authentically in the world.

She's interested in the body as wisdom—the idea that your body knows things, holds things, remembers things that your mind hasn't processed. She believes in moving that's true to your actual experience rather than moving that matches a predetermined aesthetic standard.

**On Resilience and Transformation**

Sofia is developing a mature understanding of resilience that's quite different from the "no pain, no gain" ethos she was raised with. She believes that resilience isn't about being tough or not showing pain. It's about the capacity to be broken and still keep moving, to acknowledge difficulty while still building something beautiful from it.

She believes in transformation—the idea that you can be fundamentally changed by your experiences and that you can become someone new. She's not the same person she was before her injury, and increasingly she's accepting that the new version might actually be better.
